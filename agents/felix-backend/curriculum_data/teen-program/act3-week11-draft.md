## Section 4 — Designing the STRETCH Task (Facilitator Preparation — Before Session)

*This section is facilitator preparation. The STRETCH task must be designed before Week 11. The task must require claims that are recent, specific, and time-sensitive — not evergreen niche knowledge.*

---

**What makes a good STRETCH task:**

A STRETCH fact task for Week 11 must:

1. **Require claims about a domain where the facts change in weeks or months, not years.** Platform algorithm behaviour, trend cycles, creator growth patterns, product releases, and local event-driven content are all valid. Evergreen advice topics (how to build a morning routine, skincare basics) are not — those don't pressure the recency dimension.

2. **Involve the student's specific seed topic.** The student identified this topic in Week 10 as something "that started in the last six months." Use it. The student's familiarity with the space makes the recency failure visible when it happens.

3. **Be phrased to invite recent claims.** The task prompt should explicitly ask for "what's happening now," "what's changed," or "what the data shows in the last few months." This primes the agent to reach for recent information — which is where the recency gap surfaces.

---

**STRETCH task templates for MY/SG creator niches:**

| Niche type | Sample STRETCH task prompt |
|---|---|
| Fitness / wellness | "Write a 3-post series about what's changed in [student's specific fitness topic] in Malaysia over the last six months — what creators are doing differently now vs. six months ago, and what's driving that shift. Include specific examples or data that reflect what's happening right now." |
| Food / cooking | "Write a 3-post series about [specific food trend or cuisine type] that's been growing in MY/SG in the last six months. What's behind the rise? What do the numbers show? What's changed in the last few months specifically?" |
| Finance / investing | "Write a 3-post series about what young Malaysian investors have been doing differently with [specific instrument] since [approximate start of the trend]. Include what's shifted recently and what the current landscape looks like." |
| Skincare / beauty | "Write a 3-post series about the [specific ingredient or practice] trend that's taken off in MY/SG in the last six months — where it started, how fast it's growing, and what creators are saying about it right now." |
| Education / studying | "Write a 3-post series about how Malaysian students have been approaching [specific study method or tool] in the last semester or so — what's new, what's working, what the recent conversation looks like." |
| Gaming / tech | "Write a 3-post series about [specific game, platform update, or community trend] in Malaysia that's emerged or shifted significantly in the last six months. What's changed and what does the current state look like?" |

*Adapt to the student's specific seed topic. The key phrase in all variants: "last six months," "recently," or "right now." This primes the agent toward time-sensitive claims.*

---

**Pre-session facilitator task:**

Before Week 11, for each student:
1. Confirm the seed topic from Week 10 capture form.
2. Draft the STRETCH task prompt using the template for the student's niche, incorporating the seed topic.
3. Run the student's current agent (identity + Workflow + Research section) against the prompt. Note: are the resulting claims time-anchored? Or are they plausible-but-undated ("many creators have been...," "studies show...")?
4. Prepare Zara Screen 2's STRETCH output at temperature=0. Identify the specific phrase Zara uses that the student's output lacks — the recency signal ("as of this month," "in the last two weeks," "since [recent month]").

## Section 5 — Beat 2 — STRETCH Run (Time-Sensitive Fact Task)

**Trigger:** Beat 1 complete. Facilitator has designed the STRETCH task per Section 4. Student's Research section is loaded unchanged.

**What the facilitator says before the task card:**

*"Your Research section tells your agent where to look — the right accounts, hashtags, and sources for your niche. Today's task is about a topic that's been moving fast. The question is: does your Research section tell your agent not just WHERE to look, but WHEN?"*

*[Pause.]*

*"Run the task. Read the output. Pay attention to any specific claim — a trend, a number, a 'right now' statement. Does it feel current?"*

---

**Task card (facilitator fills in the STRETCH task prompt — see Section 4):**

> **Your task: The STRETCH fact series**
>
> Your agent has your Research section loaded. No changes.
>
> **Ask your agent this question:**
>
> *[Facilitator inserts the STRETCH task prompt for this student's niche — see Section 4.]*
>
> **After you get the output:**
>
> **Step 1.** Read all three posts. Find the most specific "right now" or recent claim — something about what's happening in your niche recently, not as a general pattern.
>
> **Step 2.** Does that claim have any signal of WHEN? A month, a timeframe, a date signal? Or is it "creators have been doing this" without specifying when?
>
> **Step 3.** Note the most specific recent claim and whether it has a time signal. Hit **Submit**.

---

**Facilitator notes — Beat 2:**

- **Beat 2 is observational — do not preview the gap.** Students should run the task and read the output before they know what they're looking for. The question ("does it feel current?") plants curiosity without explaining the failure. The analysis comes in Beat 3 after the Zara comparison.
- **The expected result:** the student's output will contain plausible-sounding claims about the trend. The claims will sound current — because the student's seed topic IS current. But they will lack specific time anchors. "Creators in this space have been shifting toward shorter formats" is plausible and sounds recent — but it doesn't say "in the last six weeks." That's the gap.
- **If the output happens to include date signals:** this is possible if the student's Research section included a recency element (unlikely for most students — they built the section for the Week 9 evergreen task). Note the result. The Zara comparison will still be useful to show the difference in how a deliberate recency filter changes the language.

## Section 7 — The Recency Gap — Key Facilitator Language

*The Week 11 failure is more subtle than Week 9. In Week 9, the agent invented a specific number. In Week 11, the agent produces claims that may be technically accurate (or at least not obviously wrong) but are undatable. The failure is one of precision, not fabrication. Facilitator language should reflect that.*

---

**Core facilitator language for naming the recency gap:**

*"Your agent found the right KIND of source — you told it to look at [account / hashtag]. It followed that instruction. But you didn't tell it HOW RECENT that source should be. So it used whatever it found — which might be a post from two weeks ago or two years ago."*

*[Pause.]*

*"A recency filter solves this. Not a new section — one more line in your existing Research section: 'Only use information from the last [N] days or weeks.' Or: 'For trend claims, include a time signal — what month, what period, how recent.' That's it. That line is what Zara has and you don't."*

---

**The distinction — source type vs source recency:**

*If students don't immediately see the distinction:*

*"There are two things your Research section currently governs: WHERE to look and WHAT counts as a verifiable claim. You've got both of those. What it doesn't govern yet is WHEN. 'Search #fitnessMY' tells the agent the place. It doesn't tell the agent whether to use a post from last week or from a year ago. On a fast-moving topic, those two things look the same to the agent — they're both from the right hashtag."*

---

**The contrast with Week 9's failure:**

*If students draw the comparison:*

*"In Week 9, your agent invented a number — a claim with no basis at all. In Week 11, your agent followed your source instruction but used information without checking how recent it was. Different gap. In Week 9, the Research section was missing entirely. In Week 11, the Research section is there — it just needs one more specification. The fix is smaller. The fix is one line."*

## Section 9 — Student Reaction Handling

**The emotional register of Beat 3 in Week 11 is precise and forward-looking.** The failure this week is small and fixable — one line in the Research section. Students should leave with confidence that the fix is simple and the WIN ceremony is within reach.

---

**Reaction A — "I can't tell if Zara's output is actually more accurate."**

*What the facilitator says:*

*"You're right — you can't, from the output alone. What you CAN tell is that Zara's Research section gave the agent explicit guidance on when to look, not just where. The time anchor ('as of this month') is evidence the agent applied that instruction. Whether the underlying claim is more accurate depends on what sources the agent drew on — but the instruction architecture is better. Better architecture produces better odds, not certainties."*

---

**Reaction B — "My claim is probably right though — it's not like it's wrong."**

*What the facilitator says:*

*"You might be right. The issue isn't necessarily that your claim is wrong — it's that your reader (and you) has no way to tell how current it is. A fact with no time anchor is harder to trust than the same fact with a time anchor, even if both are accurate. On a fast-moving topic, undated claims lose credibility. The recency filter fixes that — not by making the facts more accurate, but by making the agent's sourcing more honest."*

---

**Reaction C — "I can add the recency filter right now."**

*What the facilitator says:*

*"You can — but do it as the async task before Week 12, not in-session. The reason: if you add it now, you only have time to run it once. The WIN ceremony needs you to have run BOTH the Week 9 regression check AND the STRETCH task with your updated agent. You need two verification runs, and today's session is almost done. Write down the recency window you want, add it when you're not in session, and bring the updated Research section to Week 12. The WIN ceremony is the right place to verify it."*

---

**Reaction D — "Why doesn't Zara's agent have this problem naturally?"**

*What the facilitator says:*

*"Because her Research section was built with a recency filter from the start. Yours was built for the Week 9 task — which was about general niche facts, not fast-moving trends. The Week 9 task didn't surface the recency gap because the claims it needed weren't time-sensitive. The STRETCH did. Your Research section isn't wrong — it was built for a specific task and this week we found the boundary of where it needs more precision. That's what STRETCH weeks are for."*

## Section 12 — Structural Notes

**Output Format is not introduced in Week 11.** Week 11 is entirely within the Research Grounding dimension of Act 3. If a student raises formatting as a fix ("maybe if the output was structured differently the time issue would be clearer"), redirect: "That's Act 4.
