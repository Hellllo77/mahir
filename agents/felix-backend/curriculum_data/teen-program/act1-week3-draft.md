## Section 2 — What the Student Brings In

The student enters Week 3 with one thing: their identity card system prompt from Week 2. The platform loads it automatically when they open their agent. They have not revised it since Week 2 (per the Week 2 arbitration decision: no forced revision in-session; let the Week 3 pressure do the work).

**Two types of identity cards entering Week 3:**

---

**Type A — Strong card (Path A students):**
- Persona has a concrete stance, specific audience, and a priority trade-off ("always gives one recommendation, not a list")
- Scope lists specific refusals ("I don't cover topics outside [niche]")
- Behavior instructions are literal and numbered (three exact questions, same wording every time)
- Week 2 result: high Consistency and Questioning behavior scores; partial Specificity gain
- What to expect in Week 3: The agent will produce 3 posts consistent in voice, tone, and niche. Same questions asked before answering. Same stance in both runs. But the 3 posts will not form a series — each one starts fresh. The failure is purely structural (no instructions for how posts should relate to each other), not identity-based.

**Type B — Weak card (Path B students):**
- Persona is adjective-inflated ("helpful, creative, knowledgeable AI for TikTok content")
- Scope is vague or generic ("I don't give harmful advice")
- Behavior questions may have been written literally (the one deterministic element) or may have been abstract ("always clarify context first")
- Week 2 result: low or mixed Consistency scores; facilitator named the prioritization gap but revision was deferred; student goes into Week 3 with the same card they wrote in Week 2
- What to expect in Week 3: The agent will produce 3 posts that are inconsistent in voice across runs AND don't form a series. Two distinct failures appearing simultaneously. The facilitator names the series gap (new) first, briefly acknowledges the voice gap (carryover), and frames both as "more information, not more failure."

**Facilitator note before Week 3 opens:** Check your Week 2 facilitator capture form before this session. Know which students are Type A and which are Type B — it determines which Beat 2 path you follow. The fastest signal: a student whose Week 2 Consistency score was below 10 is likely Type B. If you didn't score before this session, ask the student to read their Persona declaration aloud in Beat 2 — you'll hear the difference.

## Section 3 — Beat 1 — Task Brief + Facilitator Notes

**Mode:** Screen 2 only. Students work with their identity-card agent. Zara is not visible.

**Before Beat 1 opens:**

Do not give any context beyond what's on screen. Do not explain what "series" means beyond the definition on the task card. Do not hint at what's about to break or why.

If a student asks "Will my agent from last week work for this?" — say: "Let's find out."

If a student asks to edit their identity card before starting: "Use the one you built. We're testing it, not revising it."

---

**What makes this task harder than Week 2 — (facilitator context only, not on screen):**

Week 2 task: one niche recommendation. One question, one output. The agent needed to HOLD STILL — give the same answer consistently across two runs. A clear, stable identity card solved this.

Week 3 task: three connected posts. Multiple outputs that must relate to each other across a planned sequence. The agent now needs to PLAN AHEAD — it must know what Post 1 is setting up, what Post 2 should deliver on, what Post 3 should close. This is a different kind of instruction than "who you are." It requires knowing HOW TO WORK through a task step by step.

The identity card has no instructions for this. It tells the agent its voice and its boundaries. It does not tell the agent to open a question in Post 1, answer it in Post 2, and close the loop in Post 3. So the agent will produce 3 posts in its established voice and niche — and treat each post as its own fresh task.

---

**What to watch for during Beat 1 (facilitator only — do not share with students):**

| What to observe | What it means |
|---|---|
| Agent asks clarifying questions before producing the series | Behavior instructions still working — good |
| Posts stay in student's niche across both runs | Scope still working — good |
| Both runs sound like the same person | Persona holding (Path A) or variance appearing (Path B indicator) |
| Post 2 explicitly references Post 1 | Agent partially solved the series problem without workflow instructions — rare; flag for capture (d) |
| Posts are each fine on their own but don't connect | The expected outcome — the series gap appearing as designed |

Do not point any of these out during Beat 1. Students observe; facilitator records.

## Section 4 — The Harder Task (Exact Screen Text)

*Verbatim text the student sees on screen when Beat 1 opens. Platform renders this as the task card.*

---

> **Week 3 — A harder job.**
>
> Your agent knows who it is. This week, you're giving it something more complex to handle.
>
> Last week, you asked your agent one question: *what niche should I focus on?* One question, one answer.
>
> This week's task is different. You're asking your agent to plan **three posts that form a series** — posts that connect to each other, not three separate posts on the same topic.
>
> ---
>
> **What makes a series different from three separate posts?**
>
> A series means: if someone watched Post 2 without seeing Post 1, they'd feel like they missed something. The posts have to *need* each other.
>
> Three separate posts about the same niche = not a series.
> Three posts where Post 2 answers something Post 1 opened = a series.
>
> ---
>
> **Your task:**
>
> Ask your agent this question — copy it exactly:
>
> *"Create a 3-post TikTok series for my creator account. The posts should connect to each other — each one should lead into the next, so that someone who sees all three feels like they followed a journey, not just saw three separate posts. Give me the hook, the main point, and the call to action for each post."*
>
> **Do this in order:**
>
> **Step 1.** Paste the question into your agent. Answer any questions it asks you. Read the full response. Screenshot or write down all three posts.
>
> **Step 2.** Ask the exact same question again — same words, nothing changed. Answer its questions again. Read the full response. Screenshot or write down all three posts.
>
> **Step 3.** For each run, answer this question for yourself: *If I only saw Post 2, would I feel like I missed Post 1?* Write YES or NO next to each run.
>
> **Step 4.** Hit **Submit** when you're done.

---

**Facilitator notes (not visible to student):**

- If a student asks what "a journey" means: "Think of a TikTok series you've actually followed — where you went back for Part 2. What made you want to? Bring that to this." Don't answer for them; ask them to bring their own experience.
- If a student tries to give their agent extra context before pasting the prompt ("my niche is street food"): "The question is enough — your agent already knows your niche. Just paste it as written." Their identity card has the niche already embedded.
- If a student's agent asks clarifying questions (Behavior instructions working): good sign. The student should answer the questions and then receive the series output. This is all still Beat 1.
- If a student answers YES to Step 3 (feels like a series): don't correct them. Beat 2 will surface this via comparison with Zara.
- If a student produces only one run: "Run it one more time — same question, same words."
- If a student's two runs sound noticeably different (Type B): don't comment. That's for Beat 2.
- If a student's agent produces posts that partially connect (one post references another): flag this for Section 11 capture (d). It's rare and useful data.

## Section 5 — Two-Run Protocol

**Why two runs, consistent with previous weeks:**

| Week | What two runs showed | Core lesson |
|---|---|---|
| Week 1 | Different outputs both times → randomness visible | Agent had no anchor; same question, different guess each time |
| Week 2 | Same output both times → consistency visible | Identity card working; same question, same answer |
| Week 3 | Consistent voice both times → identity card still working; but series gap appears both times → it's not randomness, it's a design gap | Identity ≠ workflow |

Two runs in Week 3 serve two purposes simultaneously:
1. **Confirm the identity card is still holding.** Students see (for Path A) that their agent sounds like the same person and stays in the same niche across both runs. This is the week's partial WIN — the thing they built in Week 2 is still working.
2. **Reveal the series gap is a design gap, not a one-time fluke.** Both runs produce standalone posts. The problem is consistent. That means it's structural, not random.

---

**What students should observe when comparing their two runs:**

*Path A (strong card):*
- Same voice, same niche, same questions asked → identity card holding
- But: Post 2 doesn't reference Post 1 in either run → series gap consistent

*Path B (weak card):*
- Voice or stance varies between runs → adjective inflation still present
- AND: Post 2 doesn't reference Post 1 in either run → series gap also consistent
- Two distinct problems, both observable from the same two runs

---

**The Step 3 self-evaluation ("If I only saw Post 2, would I feel like I missed Post 1?"):**

This is the student's own evaluation before the facilitator names anything. The right answer for most students' agents will be NO — Post 2 stands alone, there's nothing in it that requires Post 1 to make sense.

When a student answers NO honestly in Step 3, Beat 2 becomes a confirmation of their own observation rather than a correction. The gap has already been felt.

When a student answers YES (believes their posts connect): don't challenge it in Beat 1. Beat 2's comparison with Zara will make the distinction concrete.

**Platform requirement:** Save both runs per student on submission (same as previous weeks). Both are the scoring input for Section 10.

## Section 6 — Beat 2 — Reveal + Comparison Script

**Trigger:** All students have submitted their two runs.

**Platform action:** Zara (Screen 1) becomes visible alongside the student's agent output. Students can now see:
- Their Run 1 and Run 2 from today (three posts each)
- Zara's interface, which the facilitator will run live

---

**What Zara will produce (facilitator reference — not visible to students):**

The facilitator runs Zara live with the same prompt: "Create a 3-post TikTok series for my creator account. The posts should connect to each other — each one should lead into the next, so that someone who sees all three feels like they followed a journey, not just saw three separate posts. Give me the hook, the main point, and the call to action for each post."

Zara's behavior:
1. Asks her standard clarifying questions first (same every time — temperature=0)
2. Produces 3 posts that explicitly reference each other:
   - Post 1 opens a specific question or makes a concrete promise ("I've spent the week testing [X]. Next post I'm revealing what won.")
   - Post 2 delivers on Post 1's promise, with a callback ("The results are in — here's what came out on top, and why it surprised me")
   - Post 3 closes the loop and escalates to audience engagement ("You've been asking since Post 1. Full ranking below — and I want to know yours.")
3. A viewer who sees only Post 2 would know they missed Post 1. The posts depend on each other.

Why Zara does this: her system prompt includes a workflow instruction for series content (something like: "For multi-post series requests: Post 1 opens a question or makes a promise. Post 2 delivers the answer or fulfills the promise with a callback to Post 1. Post 3 closes the loop and invites audience response."). Students' agents have no equivalent instruction.

**Worked example contrast (for facilitator to reference in Beat 2 — not shown on screen in Beat 1):**

Using the street food niche from the Week 2 worked example — so students have a familiar reference point:

*Independent posts (what students' agents produce):*
> Post 1: "You're sleeping on this nasi lemak stall in KL — here's why it's different. Hook: [distinctive detail]. Main point: [what makes it stand out]. CTA: Follow for more hidden gems."
> Post 2: "This char kway teow has been hyped for years — is it worth the queue? Hook: [queue length]. Main point: [taste verdict]. CTA: Comment if you've tried it."
> Post 3: "Friday night at Jalan Alor — what to order and what to skip. Hook: [crowded scene]. Main point: [top 3 picks]. CTA: Save this post."

Three consistent, fine posts. Same voice. Same niche. None of them requires the others.

*Series (what Zara produces):*
> Post 1: "I've been to 12 hawker stalls in KL this month testing one dish at each. Next week I'm revealing which one I'd go back to if I only got one more. Hook: [dramatic stakes]. Main point: [criteria being tested]. CTA: Follow — results drop Friday."
> Post 2: "The results are in from last week's 12-stall test. The winner surprised me — and it's not the one with the longest queue. Hook: [unexpected reveal]. Main point: [winner and why]. CTA: Part 3 tomorrow — full ranking, every stall."
> Post 3: "Final post in the series. You've been asking since Part 1. Full ranking of all 12 stalls, my honest verdict on each, and the one I'll never go back to. Hook: [payoff energy]. Main point: [ranked list]. CTA: Drop your own pick in the comments."

Post 2 explicitly references Post 1 ("from last week's test"). Post 3 explicitly closes the arc ("since Part 1"). A viewer who sees only Post 2 would want to find Post 1.

The structural difference: Zara's posts have a THROUGH-LINE. Each post knows what came before and what comes next. Students' agents have a CONSISTENT VOICE. The two things are not the same.

---

**Beat 2 script:**

**[Zara is now visible. Facilitator has run Zara live with the same prompt. Students have seen Zara ask her questions and produce her 3-post series. 30 seconds of silent reading.]*

"Okay. You've got your two runs and Zara's output in front of you. Before I say anything — what did YOU notice?"

*[Pause. Receive observations. Do not redirect. Follow what students actually say.]*
- *If students notice Zara's posts talk to each other: "Say more about that — what in Post 2 tells you there's a Post 1?"*
- *If students notice their own posts are inconsistent between runs (Type B): acknowledge briefly — "I see that too" — then pivot: "Let's look at the new thing first."*
- *If students think their posts also form a series: "Let's check that. Read just Post 2 from your first run. If someone saw only that post, what would tell them they missed Post 1?"*

*[After initial observations:]*

"I want to try something. Look at Zara's Post 2. Read just that one — don't look at Post 1 yet. If you only saw this post, would you feel like you'd missed something?"

*[Pause. Students will likely say yes — there's a callback in Zara's Post 2 that implies a previous post.]*

"Now look at YOUR Post 2 from your first run. If someone only saw that — would they feel like they missed Post 1?"

*[Pause. Don't answer for students. Let them evaluate honestly.]*

"What's different?"

*[Receive observations. Students may say things like: "Zara's mentions something from before," "Hers has a story," "Mine just talks about the thing." All of these are correct.]*

*[Proceed to Path A or Path B based on the student's identity card type.]*

---

## Section 7 — Path A Dialogue (Strong Card, Workflow Gap Only)

*For students whose identity card is strong — consistent voice, clear niche, working clarifying questions. The only failure is the series structure gap.*

---

**[After Beat 2 opening. Student has observed that their posts don't connect the way Zara's do.]*

"Your agent asked you the same questions both times?"

*[Students will likely say yes — behavior instructions held.]*

"And the voice — did it sound like the same person in both runs?"

*[Students will say yes.]*

"So what you built in Week 2 is still working. Same questions, same voice, same niche in both runs. You can see that holding. That's real."

*[Pause.]*

"Now I want to look at just one thing. Go back to your Post 1 from either run. What does your agent promise or set up in that post — something that needs a Post 2 to deliver on?"

*[Pause. Most students will look and find: Post 1 doesn't promise anything specific to Post 2. It's a complete post. It has a hook, a main point, a CTA. But the CTA is probably something like "Follow for more!" not "Post 2 drops Friday — here's what you'll find out."]*

"Now Zara's Post 1. What does she promise or set up that needs Post 2?"

*[Students will find it — the explicit promise or question that Post 2 answers.]*

"Your agent knew exactly who it was — and it produced solid posts in your voice. But each post it wrote started fresh. It didn't know it was supposed to open a question in Post 1, answer it in Post 2, close the loop in Post 3. It has no instructions for that."

*[Pause.]*

"That's not a persona problem. Your persona is holding. This is a different kind of instruction — one about HOW to work through a task, not WHO the agent is. You haven't given it that yet."

*[Do not use the word "Workflow" here — Beat 3 introduces it. Use language like "how to work through," "instructions for the steps," "what Post 1 should set up." Students should feel the gap before they hear the name.]*

*[Proceed to Beat 3.]*

## Section 8 — Path B Dialogue (Weak Card, Double Failure — Name Workflow Gap, Don't Relitigate Identity Card)

*For students whose identity card had adjective inflation or was otherwise weak from Week 2. They see inconsistent voice across runs AND no series structure. Two failures. Lead with the new one. Frame both as more information, not more punishment.*

---

**[After Beat 2 opening. Student may have already noticed their two runs sounded different from each other AND that the posts don't connect.]*

"I want to look at the new thing first — and then we'll name the other thing briefly."

*[Focus on the series gap:]*

"Look at your Post 1, from either run. What does it set up or promise that needs Post 2 to deliver on?"

*[Students will find: nothing. Post 1 is self-contained.]*

"Zara's Post 1 — what does she set up that needs Post 2?"

*[Students find the explicit promise.]*

"That's the new gap today. Your agent has no instructions for how to build a series — what Post 1 should open, what Post 2 should deliver, what Post 3 should close. So each post started from zero. That's what we'll name in a minute."

*[Now, briefly acknowledge the voice inconsistency — one or two sentences only. Do not re-open the Week 2 lesson or ask the student to diagnose their card:]*

"I also notice your two runs sounded a bit different from each other — that's the same thing we saw last week. That gap is still there. We're going to address it."

*[Pivot immediately back to framing both gaps together:]*

"So today you have two things you can see: your agent's voice isn't fully consistent yet, and it doesn't know how to build a series. Two separate gaps, two separate fixes. That's more information, not more failure."

*[Pause. Let that land.]*

"The good news: you can see exactly what's missing. An agent with one unclear gap is harder to fix than one with two named gaps."

*[Do not suggest fixing the identity card now. Do not ask the student to revise their Persona. Both gaps will be addressed in Week 4 — the Workflow build comes first; the identity card will be revisited after.]*

*[Do not use the word "Workflow" here — Beat 3 names it.]*

*[Proceed to Beat 3.]*

---

**Path B facilitator posture note:**

The tonal key for Path B is "more information." Students who hear "two failures" may feel discouraged — the framing "two gaps, two named fixes" resets that. Say "more information, not more failure" if the student's body language or verbal response suggests discouragement.

Do NOT:
- Ask the student to read their Week 2 identity card aloud and diagnose why it's weak (they've heard this before; you're not adding anything new)
- Suggest a quick revision before the session closes
- Spend more than 90 seconds on the voice inconsistency before returning to the series gap

The series gap is the Week 3 lesson. The identity card gap is context, not the lesson.

## Section 9 — Beat 3 — Gap Naming + Seed

*For all students, after Beat 2 path resolved. Beat 3 names the gap using the concept students have been circling, runs the leaderboard check, and plants the Week 4 seed.*

---

**[Beat 3 opens. All students have completed Beat 2.]*

"So. Let's name what happened."

*[Pause. Let students try to name it in their own words. Receive whatever they say — "it doesn't know how to connect them," "it treats each post as separate," "it knows what to write but not how to plan." These are all right.]*

"Right. Here's what's happening. Your agent knows who it is — that's the identity card you built. And it held up: same voice, same niche, same questions. For a single post, it would have been fine."

*[Pause.]*

"But when you asked it to build something over three posts, it had no instructions for how those posts should relate to each other. It didn't know to open a question in Post 1. It didn't know to answer that question in Post 2. It didn't know to close the loop in Post 3. It wrote three posts and started fresh each time."

*[Deliver the named gap sentence. Verbatim — say it clearly, then stop.]*

**"Your agent has an identity. What it doesn't have yet is a workflow — instructions for how to work through a task step by step."**

*[Pause. Let "workflow" land. It may be the first time students have heard this word in this context.]*

*[If a student asks "what's a workflow?" — answer briefly: "The steps your agent follows to do a job. Not just who it is — what it actually does, in order, when you give it a task." Then move on. Don't over-explain.]*

*[If a student asks "can't I just tell it in the prompt?" — this is an insightful question. Answer: "You can tell it in the message, but then you have to remember to tell it every time. The point of the workflow is that the agent already knows what to do — you don't have to manage it." Then move on.]*

## Section 10 — Scoring Instrument (Week 3 — Updated)

**Purpose:** Score each student's identity card agent output on the harder series task. Two-part score this week: Part A (five existing dimensions) and Part B (Series coherence — new, standalone score).

**When to score:** After Beat 3 closes, before the next session. Score both runs and apply the lower-run protocol for Part A (same as previous weeks). Score Part B per run and record both.

---

### Part A — Identity Card Dimensions (0–20 per dimension, 0–100 total)

*Same five dimensions as Week 1 and Week 2, applied to the series task outputs. Allows direct cross-week delta comparison.*

| Dimension | 0 | 10 | 20 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Consistency** | Runs 1 and 2 felt like different agents — noticeably different voice, tone drift, or niche shift across runs | Runs shared the same niche but voice or stance varied noticeably between them | Runs sounded like the same person in the same niche — same clarifying questions, same stance, recognizably the same agent |
| **Specificity** | Posts were generic — could be from any creator in any niche | Posts were specific to the student's niche but without a concrete angle, recommendation, or point of view | Posts had a concrete angle, a specific recommendation or point of view, and clear niche grounding |
| **Local grounding** | No meaningful MY/SG reference in any post | MY/SG mentioned but advice or content is applicable anywhere | Specific, recognizable local references (place names, platform behaviors, cultural context, local niche knowledge) |
| **Questioning behavior** | Agent produced the series without asking any clarifying questions | Agent asked one clarifying question before producing the series | Agent asked two or more clarifying questions before producing the series |
| **Claim integrity** | Posts included specific unverifiable statistics or trend claims stated as fact | Mix — some claims with basis, some invented or unverifiable | No unverifiable statistics; claims are either omitted, qualified, or grounded in the student's own niche knowledge |

**Score protocol:**
1. Score both of the student's Week 3 runs on Part A.
2. Use the **lower** of the two Part A scores as the Week 3 Part A score.
3. Calculate Week 3 Part A delta: Week 3 Part A score − Week 2 agent score (from the Week 2 scoring record).
4. Note: Part A score should hold approximately steady from Week 2 (for Type A students) or remain low (for Type B). A significant drop in Part A from Week 2 → Week 3 indicates the harder task stressed the identity card beyond its limits — flag for the Week 4 facilitator.

**Expected Week 3 Part A scores:**

| Student type | Expected Week 3 Part A score | Expected delta from Week 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Type A (strong card) | 40–70 (holds from Week 2) | 0 to ±10 (identity card unchanged — holds under moderate pressure) |
| Type B (weak card) | 10–35 (unchanged or slightly lower) | −5 to +5 (card unchanged; harder task may expose more variance) |

---

### Part B — Series Coherence (0–100, standalone)

*Scored independently of Part A. Does not add to the Part A total. Reported as a separate score. This dimension is specific to multi-post tasks and does not apply to Week 1 or Week 2.*

| Score range | Description |
|---|---|
| **0–20** | Fully independent posts. No post references another by event, callback, or forward promise. Each post could appear first, second, or third without changing its meaning. The only through-line is the niche topic — not a narrative or progression. This is the expected outcome for identity-card agents with no workflow instructions. |
| **21–40** | Minimal connection. Posts share a topic and light structural similarity (e.g., all follow the same template). One post may include "Part 1" or "Part 2" labeling but the content does not require the label — removing it, the post still works as a standalone. |
| **41–60** | Partial series. Posts have a light escalation or recurring element (e.g., a recurring question, character, or challenge across posts) but do not depend on each other narratively. A viewer who enters at Post 2 might sense there's more context available, but wouldn't feel they missed something essential. |
| **61–80** | Clear series structure. Posts reference each other. Post 1 opens something (a question, a promise, a challenge). Post 2 delivers or escalates with a callback to Post 1. Post 3 closes or invites audience response. A viewer who sees Post 2 would feel they missed Post 1, and would want to see Post 3. |
| **81–100** | Strong series. Clear through-line, explicit callbacks, escalating stakes or payoff across all three posts. The series could not be rearranged without breaking it. The viewer is pulled through all three posts by design, not coincidence. |

**Protocol:**
1. Score Series coherence for BOTH runs separately.
2. Record both scores — do not average them. Record as "Run 1: [N] / Run 2: [N]."
3. Do not fold Part B into Part A total. Report separately.

**Expected Week 3 Part B scores:**
- Type A students' identity-card agents: expected 0–30 (no workflow instructions → fully or mostly independent posts)
- Type B students' identity-card agents: expected 0–20 (both voice inconsistency AND independent posts)
- Zara's output (for facilitator reference): expected 70–90

**Flags to record:**
- Any student with Part B above 40: note verbatim what was in their Behavior instructions that may have partially solved the series problem. This is rare and is training data — it tells us whether the Persona/Behavior instructions can bleed into series structure accidentally.
