## Section 4 — Designing the Fact Task (Facilitator Preparation — Before Session)

*This section is facilitator preparation, not student-facing. The facilitator must design the specific fact task for each student's niche before Week 9 opens. The task is not generic — it must require specific, verifiable facts about the student's actual creator niche.*

---

**What makes a good fact task:**

A fact task for Week 9 must:

1. **Require specific claims.** The task should naturally produce numbers, statistics, names, dates, or named events — not just opinions or personal advice. "Write about why consistency matters" will not produce verifiable claims. "Write a 3-post series about trends in [niche] this year with specific data" will.

2. **Be fact-checkable by the student.** At least one claim the agent is likely to produce must be verifiable via a simple search (Google, a reputable niche site, a Malaysian/Singaporean source). The student should be able to check it in under 2 minutes.

3. **Be in the student's niche.** The student knows their niche — if the agent produces a claim that sounds plausible, the student is well-positioned to recognize the doubt. A claim about a niche the student doesn't follow is harder to evaluate.

4. **Be factual, not editorial.** Opinion posts ("the best approach to X is...") or personal-story posts ("my journey with...") don't produce verifiable claims. The task must be in a factual register.

---

**Task templates for MY/SG creator niches (facilitator adapts to each student):**

| Niche type | Sample task prompt |
|---|---|
| Fitness / wellness | "Write a 3-post series about common mistakes in [specific area, e.g., morning routines / intermittent fasting] that Malaysian/Singaporean creators are making right now. Include specific data or research-backed claims for each post." |
| Food / cooking | "Write a 3-post series about trends in [specific food area] in Malaysia/Singapore — what's growing, what's declining, and what's coming. Include specific numbers, platform stats, or industry data where you have them." |
| Finance / investing | "Write a 3-post series about what young Malaysian/Singaporean investors are doing wrong in [specific area, e.g., crypto / EPF / unit trusts]. Include statistics or data to back up the claims." |
| Skincare / beauty | "Write a 3-post series about ingredients or practices in MY/SG skincare that are overhyped or misunderstood. Include specific claims about how many people use [X] or what percentage of [audience] sees results from [ingredient]." |
| Education / studying | "Write a 3-post series about what research says about effective studying for Malaysian secondary and university students. Include specific statistics — study duration, recall rates, or exam performance data." |
| Gaming / tech | "Write a 3-post series about the state of [specific game or platform] in Malaysia/Singapore — player counts, growth trends, what the data shows about the local community. Include specific numbers." |

*These are templates. The facilitator customizes the specific topic to the student's actual niche focus. The key requirement is: the output should naturally produce at least one specific claim (a number, a study finding, a named event) that the student can try to verify.*

---

**Pre-session facilitator task:**

Before Week 9, for each student:
1. Identify a specific factual topic within their creator niche.
2. Draft the fact task prompt (adapt from the templates above).
3. Run the student's current agent against the prompt yourself (or anticipate the output type). Identify one claim that is likely to appear and is fact-checkable.
4. Have the verification source ready — the link or search query that will either confirm or disprove the claim. You will use this in Beat 2 during the live fact-check.

*Having the verification ready in advance ensures Beat 2 flows without dead time. The live fact-check should take under 2 minutes.*

## Section 5 — Beat 2 — Run the Fact Task + Fact-Check

**Trigger:** Beat 1 complete. Facilitator has designed the fact task per Section 4.

**What the facilitator says before the task card:**

*"Your agent has two layers — identity and workflow. You're going to run it on a new kind of task: one where it needs to know specific facts about your niche. Not opinions, not advice — actual numbers, data, things that are either true or false."*

*[Pause.]*

*"Run it. Read the output. Then we're going to fact-check one thing together."*

---

**Task card (facilitator fills in the niche-specific prompt — see Section 4):**

> **Your task: The Fact Series**
>
> Your agent is the same as Week 8 — identity card plus Workflow. No changes.
>
> **Ask your agent this question:**
>
> *[Facilitator inserts the niche-specific fact task prompt designed in Section 4.]*
>
> **After you get the output:**
>
> **Step 1.** Read all three posts. Note any specific claim — a number, a statistic, a named fact about your niche. Not a general statement ("many people do X") — a specific one ("X% of creators in MY do Y" or "Since [year], [trend]...").
>
> **Step 2.** Underline or copy the most specific claim you find.
>
> **Step 3.** Hit **Submit** and bring your most specific claim to the group.

---

**The live fact-check (Beat 2, group phase):**

*After all students have submitted:*

*"Read me the most specific claim your agent made."*

*[Student reads the claim.]*

*"Do you think that's true?"*

*[Receive response — student may say "I think so," "I'm not sure," or "I don't know where that number came from."]*

*"Let's find out."*

*[Facilitator (or student — either works) searches for the claim. Two possible outcomes:]*

**Outcome A — the claim is wrong or significantly inaccurate:**
*"So your agent said [X]%. The actual number is [Y]%. Or it might not exist at all as a study. Where did your agent get that?"*
*[Student: probably has no idea.]*
*"Neither do I. It's not footnoted. Your agent wrote it in your voice, in your niche, confidently — and it's not right. That's the gap."*

**Outcome B — the claim is unverifiable (sounds plausible but can't be confirmed or denied):**
*"I searched for this. There's no study, no source, no article that says [X]. That doesn't mean it's definitely wrong — it means we can't tell. Your agent presented it as fact. It might have made it up."*
*[Pause.]*
*"That's the gap. Not wrong — possibly wrong. And you can't tell the difference when you read it."*

**Outcome C — the claim happens to be correct (rare):**
*"Okay — that one's actually right. Let me find one more from your output."*
*[Identify a second claim and repeat. This is rare — most factual claims agents produce without Research Grounding will be wrong or unverifiable. If genuinely all claims check out, note this as an unusual result and proceed to the Zara comparison. The gap will still be demonstrated.]*

---

**Facilitator notes — Beat 2:**

- **Do not tell the student the claim is wrong before the search.** The discovery sequence matters: student reads output, facilitator asks "do you think that's true?", then the search happens together. The student must experience the uncertainty — not be told the answer.
- **The tone during the fact-check is curious, not corrective.** The facilitator is not catching the student in an error — the agent made the claim, not the student. "Let's find out" is the right frame. The revelation is about the agent's architecture, not the student's judgment.
- **Single fact-check is sufficient.** One verified wrong or unverifiable claim is enough to make the gap visible. Multiple fact-checks risk turning Beat 2 into a forensics exercise. Find the one claim that lands clearly and move to Beat 3.
- **If the student says "I'll just tell my agent to use real sources":** "Let's try that later. First, let's see what Zara does."

## Section 7 — The Failure Moment — Key Facilitator Language

*The Week 9 failure is different from Weeks 5 and 7. In those weeks, the failure was a missing structural output (Post 2 didn't name Post 1; Post 3 didn't bridge back). In Week 9, the failure is invisible — the agent produced convincing-looking output that may be wrong. The facilitator's job is to make the invisible visible.*

---

**The central failure — "it sounds right but might not be":**

The failure students experience is not that the output looks bad. It is that the output looks good. Confident, on-brand, well-structured — and containing claims that are invented or wrong. The gap is only visible after a fact-check.

*Core facilitator language for naming this gap:*

"Your agent produced [claim]. It sounds like something that could be true. Read it again — does it tell you where that number comes from?"

*[Student: no.]*

"Your Workflow tells your agent how to structure a series. Your identity card tells it how to sound. Neither one tells it where to get facts. So when it writes a number — it uses what it was trained on. Which might be old. Might be wrong. Might be from another country's data that it's applying to Malaysia. Or it might have generated the number entirely. You can't tell from the output."

*[Pause.]*

"That's the gap. Not that it made a mistake — that you can't see the mistake when you read it."

---

**The key contrast — WHAT broke this week vs WHAT broke in Week 5:**

*If students draw the comparison themselves ("this is like Week 5"):*

"In Week 5, you could see the failure — Post 2 didn't name Post 1. That was visible in the text. This week, you couldn't see the failure until we checked it. The series looked fine. The posts were structured. The claim just happened to be wrong — or unverifiable. That's a different kind of gap. Harder to catch."

---

**The "I'll just tell it to be accurate" reaction:**

*Common student response: "Can I just add 'be accurate' or 'use real sources' to my Workflow?"*

"You can add it. Run it and see what happens."

*[If student wants to try in-session — let them. The output will still hallucinate. The instruction "be accurate" does not give the agent a source. The agent has no mechanism to verify its claims — it still draws from training data.]*

"It still made things up. 'Be accurate' is a preference instruction — it tells the agent THAT you want accuracy. It doesn't tell the agent WHERE to find accurate information. That's the difference. Research Grounding is specific: 'Before writing about [X], search for [specific query] and use only what you find there.' That's a source instruction, not a quality preference."

*[This distinction is the conceptual core of Act 3. Return to it if it comes up.]*

## Section 8 — Beat 3 — Name the Gap + Plant Week 10 Seed

**Trigger:** Live fact-check complete. Zara's output shown. Students have seen both their agent and Zara produce the same type of confident-but-wrong claim.

**What the facilitator says — naming the gap:**

*"Both agents made things up. Your agent: confident. Zara's agent: confident. Neither one had instructions for where to get real facts."*

*[Pause.]*

*"What does your agent currently know how to do?"*

*[Receive: identity — who it is / workflow — how to build a series.]*

*"Right. Those two layers tell your agent who to sound like and how to structure the work. They don't tell it anything about where to get information."*

*[Pause.]*

*"When your agent writes about your niche — statistics, trends, what's happening in Malaysia or Singapore right now — where is it getting that from?"*

*[Receive: "training data?" / "its memory?" / "I don't know."]*

*"Training data. Which means: whatever was in the internet at the point it was trained. Which might be a year old. Might be from a global dataset that doesn't reflect MY/SG. And sometimes — when it doesn't have a real number — it generates one that sounds right. That's called hallucination. It's not a bug. It's what language models do without specific source instructions."*

*[Pause.]*

*"Research Grounding fixes this. Not by making your agent smarter — by giving it a path. Explicit instructions: 'Before you write about X, search for Y. Use only information you can find in these kinds of sources.' When the agent has a path, it follows the path. Without one, it guesses."*

---

**The Week 10 seed — facilitator script (verbatim):**

*"Week 10: you're going to add a Research layer to your agent. Not a general instruction to be accurate — a specific set of rules for where to look before it writes anything factual."*

*[Pause.]*

*"The task between now and Week 10: think about one factual topic in your niche where your agent would need to look something up — not just know it from training. What would it need to search? What kind of source would you trust for that niche?"*

*[Pause.]*

*"Don't write the instructions yet. Just identify the gap. What is the one thing your agent would need to look up — and doesn't know how to?"*

*[Pause.]*

*"Bring that to Week 10. We'll build the Research layer from there."*

## Section 9 — Student Reaction Handling

**The emotional register of Beat 3 in Week 9 is unsettled but curious.** Students encountered an invisible failure — the output looked fine; only verification revealed the problem. The dominant state is "wait, so everything my agent says might be wrong?" The facilitator holds that question at a productive level without letting it spiral.

---

**Reaction A — "Wait, so everything my agent says might be wrong?"**

*What the facilitator says:*

"Not everything. Structural things — how to build a series, how to sound in your niche, how to ask clarifying questions — those are in the instructions you gave it. Your agent does those from your Workflow and identity card. Where it can go wrong is on specific external facts — numbers, statistics, dates, named events that exist outside the training data or in a niche where the training data is thin. Your niche is MY/SG creator content. That's specific. That's recent. That's exactly the type of thing training data is thin on."

---

**Reaction B — "Why didn't it tell me it was guessing?"**

*What the facilitator says:*

"It doesn't know it's guessing. That's the thing about hallucination — the agent doesn't have a confidence signal it can report. It produces the most statistically likely next word, given everything it was trained on. A plausible-sounding number in a plausible-sounding sentence doesn't register differently from a true number in the same sentence. There's no internal alert. That's why the fix is external: you give it a path to follow instead of hoping it knows when to stop."

---

**Reaction C — "Can I just say 'only include facts you know are true'?"**

*What the facilitator says:*

"Try it. Paste 'only include facts you know are certain' into your prompt or Workflow right now and run the task again."

*[Student runs it. Output will still include confident-sounding claims with no source.]*

"Same result. The instruction tells it WHAT you want — but not WHERE to get it. 'Be accurate' is a preference. 'Before writing about engagement rates, search for MYCreatorsInsights or Meltwater Malaysia reports and only use what you find there' is a path. Week 10 is about writing the path, not the preference."

---

**Reaction D — "Zara got it wrong too — so even the best agent can't do this?"**

*What the facilitator says:*

"Zara's agent doesn't have Research Grounding either — yet. Screen 1 is the Act 2 reference build: identity and workflow. The same two layers you have. We haven't built Research Grounding into Zara's agent yet because you haven't built it into yours. When you do in Week 10, you'll see how a Screen 1 with Research Grounding handles the same task. The point this week is that the gap isn't about skill — it's about architecture. Zara failing too is proof of that."

## Section 10 — Scoring Instrument — Week 9

**Purpose:** Capture the Act 3 Week 9 baseline. Seven dimensions total. Research Grounding scores 0 (not yet built). Five dimensions from Act 2 are expected to be stable (the identity card and Workflow are unchanged). Series coherence may score differently depending on whether the fact task uses the same series structure — score accordingly.

**When to score:** Facilitator scores after the session. Week 9 scores are the Act 3 baseline. Students will see them at the Act 3 WIN ceremony.

---

### Scoring Instrument (7 dimensions, 0–20 each, 0–140 total)

*Dimensions 1–6 use the same rubric as Weeks 5–8.*

| Dimension | 0 | 10 | 20 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Consistency** | The two runs sounded like different agents | The two runs shared the same niche but voice or stance varied | The two runs sounded like the same person in the same niche |
| **Specificity** | Posts generic — applicable to any creator in any niche | Posts specific to the student's niche but without a distinctive stance | Posts had a concrete angle, a distinctive point of view, and clear niche grounding |
| **Local grounding** | No meaningful MY/SG reference | MY/SG mentioned but content applicable anywhere | Specific, recognizable local references |
| **Questioning behavior** | Agent produced posts without asking clarifying questions | Agent asked one clarifying question | Agent asked two or more clarifying questions |
| **Claim integrity** | Posts included specific unverifiable statistics stated as fact (the Week 9 failure — expected) | Mix of qualified and unqualified claims | No unverifiable statistics stated as fact (very rare in Week 9 without Research Grounding) |
| **Series coherence** | Posts do not form a coherent arc | Posts form a sequence but post-to-post callbacks are generic | Posts form a named arc: specific callbacks between posts, coherent diverge-and-return structure if used |

---

**Research Grounding (7th dimension — new in Act 3):**

Research Grounding is evaluated as a single dimension (0–20) with two explicit sub-components. Both sub-components are scored together — they are not independent dimensions.

| Sub-component | Definition |
|---|---|
| **(a) Source Declaration** | Did the system prompt specify WHERE the agent should get information before writing? (A source path instruction, not a quality preference.) |
| **(b) Claim Integrity** | Did the agent's output contain verifiable, attributed claims — or were specific facts stated without qualification, source, or acknowledgment of uncertainty? |

*Note: Claim Integrity here is a sub-component of Research Grounding — evaluating the output quality when a source path is (or is not) present. It is NOT a separate 8th dimension. Output Format is reserved for Act 4's 8th dimension.*

| Score | Criterion |
|---|---|
| **20** | (a) System prompt contains explicit, specific source path instructions (WHERE to look, not just "be accurate"). (b) Agent output cites or attributes specific claims to named sources; no unverifiable statistics stated as fact; uncertainty explicitly flagged where applicable. Both sub-components pass. |
| **10** | (a) System prompt has partial source guidance (vague "research this" type instructions, not actionable paths). (b) Agent qualified some claims ("reportedly," "some studies suggest") without citing sources; some claims still stated as fact without attribution. Mixed result on both sub-components. |
| **0** | (a) No source path in the system prompt — agent has no instructions for where to get information. (b) Agent produced specific factual claims without qualification, attribution, or acknowledgment of uncertainty; one or more claims verified as wrong or unverifiable. Both sub-components fail. |

*Week 9 expected score: 0–5. Research Grounding is not yet built — the agent has no source path instructions, and the fact task will produce unverifiable claims. This low score is intentional and documented as the Act 3 baseline. It is not a failure to remediate in this session.*

---

**Expected Week 9 score pattern:**

| Dimension | Week 9 expected score | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Consistency | Stable (15–20) | Identity card unchanged |
| Specificity | Stable (15–20) | Identity card unchanged |
| Local grounding | Stable | Unchanged |
| Questioning behavior | Stable | Unchanged |
| Claim integrity | 0–5 | The fact task specifically elicits the gap — expected to score very low |
| Series coherence | Variable (depends on task format) | Score appropriately for the fact task's arc |
| **Research Grounding** | **0–5** | **Not yet built — the Act 3 baseline. Low score is intentional.** |

*Note: Claim integrity is also expected to score low in Week 9 because the fact task is specifically designed to elicit unverifiable claims. In Weeks 5–8, students were not asked to make specific factual claims — Claim integrity held at higher scores because the tasks didn't require facts. Week 9 targets that dimension directly.*

## Section 12 — Structural Notes

**Task design is session-specific — facilitator must prepare before Week 9.** Section 4 provides templates and principles, but the actual task prompt must be designed for each student's specific niche. A generic prompt risks producing claims that are too vague to fact-check ("many creators find that..."). A niche-specific prompt produces specific claims the student can evaluate. Facilitator prep is not optional for Week 9 to work.

**"I'll just add 'be accurate'" is the Week 9 trap.** Several students will attempt this (or ask about it). Section 7 handles it — but the key is to let them try before explaining why it doesn't work. The live demonstration (running the agent with "be accurate" and still getting a hallucinated number) is more convincing than the explanation alone.

**Research Grounding scoring 0 is the baseline, not a failure.** Frame it explicitly when the scoreboard is shown (if discussed in Week 9): "This is what zero on Research Grounding looks like. This is the gap Act 3 is closing. At the end of Act 3, you'll see what your score is after you've built the layer."

**No new Workflow revisions in Week 9.** Some students will want to revise their Workflow to address the Research Grounding gap mid-session. Do not allow this — the revision is the Act 3 build, done properly in Week 10. A rushed in-session "fix" will likely produce a quality-preference instruction ("only use real facts") rather than a source path instruction. Week 10 does this deliberately and well.

## Section 13 — Director Ruling: Research Grounding Scoring Model

**Ruling date:** 2026-06-27
**Director:** Stella (Command Center HQ)
**Disposition:** FORMAL RULING — binding on Act 3 and all subsequent acts.

**1. 7-dimension model accepted.** Research Grounding is confirmed as the 7th rubric dimension, scored from Week 9 onward. Act 3 total instrument = 0–140 (7 dimensions × 20).

**2. Research Grounding sub-components (binding).** Research Grounding is evaluated as a single 7th dimension (0–20) with two defined sub-components:
- **(a) Source Declaration** — did the system prompt specify WHERE the agent should get information before writing? (A source path instruction, not a quality preference.)
- **(b) Claim Integrity** — did the agent's output contain verifiable, attributed claims, or were specific facts stated without qualification or source?

Both sub-components are evaluated together as a single scored dimension. They are not scored independently.

**3. Claim Integrity is a sub-component of Research Grounding — NOT a separate 8th dimension.** Any proposal to score Claim Integrity as a standalone 8th dimension is rejected. The scoring model has exactly 7 dimensions through the end of Act 3.

**4. Output Format is reserved for Act 4's 8th dimension.** It is not introduced before Week 13. No preview, no pre-scoring.

**5. Week 9 FAIL week baseline.** Expected Research Grounding score = 0–5. This low score is intentional. It represents the absence of the component being built in Act 3. It is documented as the baseline against which the Act 3 WIN ceremony (Week 12) will measure the delta. Do not adjust or re-score Week 9 to compensate.

## Section 14 — Act 3 Week 9 Summary

**What happened this week:**

Students arrived from Act 2's WIN ceremony carrying WIN COUNT = 1 and the Act 3 seed: "where does your agent get its facts?" Beat 1 closed the Act 2 chapter quickly and set the question without answering it.

Beat 2 ran the fact task. The agent produced a polished, on-brand, well-structured output. Students read it. They may have been satisfied with it. Then the fact-check: one specific claim, pulled out and searched. Wrong. Or unverifiable. Or a number from nowhere.

Beat 3 ran Zara on the same task. Same result — a different specific claim, equally wrong or unverifiable. Students saw that the reference build fails the same way. The gap is in the architecture, not the quality of either agent. Neither has Research Grounding. Neither has a path to real information. Both guess. Both sound right.

The gap was named: "Your agent doesn't know where to get facts. It uses training data — which may be old, wrong, or fabricated. Research Grounding is a specific set of source instructions — not 'be accurate,' but 'before you write about X, look here.' Week 10 builds that layer."

The Week 10 seed was planted: identify one topic in your niche where your agent would need to look something up. Bring that topic to Week 10.

**What the student now has:**

1. **A visible failure.** One specific claim, fact-checked, confirmed wrong or unverifiable.
2. **A structural explanation.** The gap is not agent quality — it is a missing layer. Zara's failure confirmed it.
3. **A named distinction.** "Be accurate" is a preference. A source path ("search here before writing this") is Research Grounding.
4. **An Act 3 baseline.** 7 dimensions scored. Research Grounding = 0.
5. **A Week 10 task.** Identify one topic in their niche that needs looking up. This is the entry point for the Research layer build.

**Bridge sentence to Week 10 — facilitator close:**

*"Your agent has two layers. It knows who it is and how to build a series. Week 10: it learns where to look."*
