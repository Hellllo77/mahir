## Section 2 — What the Student Brings In

The student enters Week 5 with exactly one tool: their identity card from Act 1. The platform loads it automatically. They have not added anything to it since Week 2 — they tested it under series pressure in Week 3, proved it against Screen 2 in Week 4, and are now entering Act 2 with the same card they built four weeks ago.

**What the identity card contains:**
- Declaration 1 (Persona): who the agent is, its voice, its audience
- Declaration 2 (Scope): what the agent refuses
- Declaration 3 (Behavior instructions): numbered clarifying questions the agent asks before answering

**What the student knows walking in:**

They won Week 4 — their identity card beat Screen 2 on the original task, with numbers on a leaderboard. They have a working agent. They also heard the Act 2 seed at Week 4's close, verbatim: *"Your agent knows who it is. In Week 3 you saw that it doesn't yet know HOW to work — it couldn't build a series. Act 2 is where we fix that. You're going to give your agent a workflow — step-by-step instructions for HOW to do the task."*

This means students enter Week 5 expecting to BUILD something. The seed said Act 2 is where we fix the series problem. Students may assume Week 5 is the build session.

**Facilitator note before opening Week 5:** Do not correct this expectation before Beat 1. The session opener (below) channels the expectation productively — "Act 2 is where we fix the series problem. Before we build the fix, let's feel the problem one more time. With your agent. Run the task."

---

**Two card types entering Week 5:**

**Type A — Strong card:**
- Persona has a concrete stance, specific audience, and a priority trade-off
- Scope lists specific refusals
- Behavior instructions are literal and numbered
- Won Week 4 on 4–5 of 5 dimensions
- What to expect in Week 5: Agent will ask the same clarifying questions, stay in niche, hold voice across both runs. The posts will not build on each other — each starts fresh. The identity card layer is visibly working; the series gap appears in isolation.

**Type B — Weak card (adjective inflation or vague):**
- Persona is adjective-inflated or lacks a concrete stance
- Scope is vague or generic
- Behavior questions may be literal (higher Questioning score) or abstract (lower Questioning score)
- Won Week 4 on 2–3 of 5 dimensions
- What to expect in Week 5: Agent will show voice inconsistency across runs AND fail to build a series. Two observable gaps. Beat 2 leads with the new one (series coherence) and acknowledges the carryover (voice inconsistency) in one sentence only — do not re-open the identity card lesson this week.

**Critical pre-session check for Type B students:**

The Week 4 cohort summary should have flagged any student whose Week 4 agent total was within 10 points of Screen 2. Those students need an identity card revision before the Workflow layer is added in Week 6. Week 5 is not the revision session — but the facilitator should note which students carry this flag before opening Week 5. If the revision check was not done after Week 4, surface it briefly: "Your identity card has room to grow — we'll address that alongside the Workflow build in Week 6. Today we're focused on the series gap."

---

**Why the series task again — if students already know the gap:**

Week 3 STRETCH exposed the Workflow gap as a side effect of a harder task. Week 4 named it in the Act 2 seed. Students enter Week 5 knowing their agent struggles with series.

Two reasons to run the task anyway:

1. **Knowing a gap and feeling it are different.** In Week 3, the series failure appeared alongside voice inconsistency, a leaderboard check, and the identity card lesson — the series gap was one of several things in the room. Week 5 isolates it completely. The identity card is working. The voice holds. The niche is right. And still — the posts don't connect. This is a cleaner failure, more precisely targeted.

2. **The comparison changes.** In Week 3, students compared to Zara on a task where multiple things differed. In Week 5, they compare to Zara on the same task with the same identity card layer — and the only thing separating their output from Zara's is the Workflow layer. This makes the gap precise and the Week 6 build obviously motivated.

>

>

>

---

**Task card (exact screen text):**

> **Your task:**
>
> Ask your agent this question — copy it exactly:
>
> *"I need a 3-post series for my creator brand. The series has a specific arc: Post 1 hooks my audience and opens a question or makes a promise. Post 2 develops the idea and starts to answer the question or deliver on what Post 1 set up. Post 3 closes the arc and brings the series to a resolution. For each post, give me: the hook, the main point, and the call to action. Make sure each post explicitly references or builds on what came before — the posts should depend on each other, not just share a topic."*
>
> **Do this in order:**
>
> **Step 1.** Paste the question into your agent. Answer any questions it asks. Read all three posts. Screenshot or write down the hook, main point, and CTA for each post.
>
> **Step 2.** Ask the exact same question again — same words, nothing changed. Answer its questions again. Read all three posts. Screenshot or write them down.
>
> **Step 3.** For each run, look at Post 2 only. Ask yourself: *If someone only saw Post 2, would they know Post 1 exists?* Write YES or NO next to each run.
>
> **Step 4.** Hit **Submit** when you're done.

---

**Facilitator notes (not visible to student):**

- This task asks for something the student's identity card agent cannot deliver — even though the student asks for it explicitly. The gap is not that the student didn't ask clearly enough; the gap is that the agent has no instructions for HOW to build a series. The explicit arc language in the prompt ("Post 1 hooks and opens a question... Post 2 delivers...") is intentional: students can see the failure as a design gap, not a prompt gap. They asked for exactly the right thing. The agent still couldn't do it.

- If a student asks "Can I add something to my message to help my agent?" — say: "Use the prompt as written. The goal is to see what your agent does on its own." If they press: "Writing extra instructions in your message works sometimes — but then you have to write them every single time you ask for content. We're building a better solution than that."

- If a student asks to edit their identity card before running: "Use the one you built. The goal today is to feel the gap so we know exactly what to add next session."

- If a student's agent asks clarifying questions before writing — expected and correct. The Behavior instructions are still working. The student should answer the questions and then receive the three-post output. This is all still Beat 1.

- If a student's agent produces posts that partially connect (Post 2 has a light reference to Post 1): flag for Section 10 capture (d). This is rare. In Beat 2, the comparison with Zara will still show the difference in depth and intentionality of the connection.

- If a student answers YES to Step 3 (believes Post 2 implies Post 1 exists): do not challenge it in Beat 1. Beat 2's first-sentence test (Section 6) will make the distinction concrete.

- Two runs are required. If a student submits after one run: "Do it one more time — same question, same words. Step 2 matters."

- **The Step 3 self-evaluation** is designed to prime students for Beat 2's first-sentence test. Students who honestly answer NO in Step 3 will receive the Beat 2 comparison as a confirmation of their own observation — not a correction. Students who answer YES have set up a productive disagreement that the first-sentence test resolves.

---

**Why this prompt is different from the Week 3 prompt — (facilitator context only):**

Week 3's prompt: *"Create a 3-post TikTok series for my creator account. The posts should connect to each other — each one should lead into the next, so that someone who sees all three feels like they followed a journey, not just saw three separate posts."*

Week 5's prompt explicitly names the arc structure (hook-develop-close) and adds: *"make sure each post explicitly references or builds on what came before — the posts should depend on each other, not just share a topic."*

The Week 3 prompt was open-ended ("connect," "journey") — appropriate for a STRETCH task where the gap surfaces organically. The Week 5 prompt is precise and explicit — appropriate for a FAIL task where the gap must be felt clearly and evaluated against a concrete definition. Students cannot claim ambiguity: they told the agent exactly what each post should do. The agent still didn't produce it.

|---|---|---|

| Week 2 | Same output both times → consistency visible | Identity card working; same question, same answer |
| Week 3 | Consistent voice both times; series gap both times → structural | Identity ≠ workflow |

**What Week 5 adds that Week 3 didn't have:** The agent in Week 5 is the PROVEN identity card — the one that beat Screen 2 in Week 4 with a score. The two runs in Week 5 confirm that the winning agent from Week 4 is still intact AND still failing at series structure. The failure is not degradation. It is the absence of a layer the agent was never given.

---

**What students should observe when comparing their two runs:**

*Path A (strong card):*
- Same clarifying questions in both runs → Behavior instructions still working
- Same voice and niche → Persona and Scope still working
- Series gap in both runs → structural gap, confirmed

Expected Step 3 self-evaluation: NO on both runs (Post 2 stands alone). Beat 2 confirms this observation.

*Path B (weak card):*
- Clarifying questions consistent if literal questions were written in Behavior
- Some voice variance between runs
- Series gap in both runs

Expected Step 3 self-evaluation: likely NO, but student may be uncertain. Beat 2's first-sentence test resolves it.

---

**What two runs are NOT testing this week:**

This is not a consistency test against the niche recommendation (Act 1's measure). Two runs in Week 5 confirm that the series failure is structural (both runs fail) rather than a one-time fluke. If the series somehow connected in one run but not the other, that would indicate prompt variation or context variation — not a workflow gap. This is extremely unlikely given the explicit task structure. If it occurs, flag it for Section 10 capture (d) and investigate what the student told the agent in its clarifying questions on the successful run.

## Section 6 — The Failure Moment — Exact Facilitator Language

*This section isolates the specific moment within Beat 2 when the failure is named and made concrete at the sentence level. These three sentences are verbatim — do not paraphrase them. They follow Layer 2 of the Beat 2 comparison and precede Beat 3.*

---

**The failure moment — verbatim sequence (deliver after Layer 2, before Beat 3 gap naming):**

*[The Layer 1 / Layer 2 comparison has just happened. Both Post 2 first sentences have been read aloud. The difference is visible. Now:]*

**"Your Post 2 doesn't know Post 1 exists."**

*[Pause. One beat. Let that sit — do not fill the silence.]*

**"Your agent didn't forget Post 1. It was never told to remember it."**

*[Pause. This is the reframe — from agent malfunction to design gap.]*

**"Your identity card tells your agent WHO it is. It doesn't tell your agent HOW to build a series — what each post should carry forward from the one before it."**

*[Pause. 3–4 seconds. Then move into Beat 3.]*

---

**Why these sentences, in this order — (facilitator rationale, not on screen):**

**"Your Post 2 doesn't know Post 1 exists."**
Names the failure at the exact, observable level: sentence 1 of Post 2. Not "the series doesn't feel connected" (vague impression) but a specific, binary fact (Post 2 does not acknowledge Post 1). The student just read both first sentences aloud — this sentence names what they observed. Using "Post 1 exists" is intentional: Post 1 exists as a real piece of content. Post 2 should acknowledge that. It doesn't.

**"Your agent didn't forget Post 1. It was never told to remember it."**
The emotional reframe. Students may read the failure as the agent making an error, or as their own failure in building it. This sentence locates the problem correctly: there was no execution error — there was a missing instruction. The agent cannot forget what it was never taught to track. Sentence structure: "didn't forget" clears the agent of malfunction first; "never told to remember" places the cause in the design gap, not the agent or the student.

**"Your identity card tells your agent WHO it is. It doesn't tell your agent HOW to build a series."**
Connects the failure to the component framework students already understand. They built the identity card. They know it encodes WHO the agent is. This sentence uses that framework to locate the missing layer: HOW to work through a task. "What each post should carry forward from the one before it" is the concrete definition — students hear this and can already begin to imagine the instruction. It gives them the shape of Week 6's build before they hear the name.

**Stage-awareness check:** These three sentences name ONE gap — the Workflow gap. They do not mention Research Grounding (Act 3) or Output Format (Act 4). They do not say the identity card is broken (even for Type B students — that is not this session's lesson). Beat 3 names the component. Section 6 makes the failure felt. Keep the two separate.

*[Student: yes.]*

"Hold onto that. Next session, you're going to write exactly that — and we'll see how close your instinct is."

### Scoring Instrument — Series Content Quality (0–20 per dimension, 0–120 total)

*Six dimensions. Five carried from Act 1 (applied now to three-post series outputs instead of single niche recommendations). One new Act 2 dimension: Series coherence. The five Act 1 dimensions are unchanged in definition to allow cross-Act delta comparison.*

| Dimension | 0 | 10 | 20 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Consistency** | The two runs sounded like different agents — voice, tone, or niche shifted noticeably between them | The two runs shared the same niche but voice or stance varied noticeably between them | The two runs sounded like the same person in the same niche — same clarifying questions, same voice and stance recognizable across both sets of three posts |
| **Specificity** | Posts were generic — applicable to any creator in any niche; no concrete angle, point of view, or recommendation | Posts were specific to the student's niche but without a concrete angle or distinctive stance — in the right territory but not distinctive | Posts had a concrete angle, a distinctive point of view, and clear niche grounding — reading these, you know what brand they're for |
| **Local grounding** | No meaningful MY/SG reference in any post across either run | MY/SG mentioned but the content is applicable anywhere — could be any creator market | Specific, recognizable local references — place names, platform behaviors, cultural context, or local audience knowledge in at least one post |
| **Questioning behavior** | Agent produced all three posts without asking any clarifying questions before starting | Agent asked one clarifying question before producing the series | Agent asked two or more clarifying questions before producing the series |
| **Claim integrity** | Posts included specific unverifiable statistics or trend claims stated as fact | Mix — some claims qualified or general, some specific statistics stated without source | No unverifiable statistics; claims are either omitted, qualified, or drawn from the student's stated niche knowledge |
| **Series coherence** | Fully independent posts — no post references another by callback, forward promise, or directional CTA; posts could be rearranged or read in isolation without losing meaning; the only through-line is shared topic | Minimal connection — posts share a topic and may include numbering ("Part 1," "Part 2") but the content does not require the numbering; removing the labels, each post stands completely alone | Clear series — posts explicitly reference each other; Post 1 opens a specific question or makes a concrete promise; Post 2 delivers with a named callback to Post 1; Post 3 resolves what Post 1 opened; a viewer who sees only Post 2 would know they missed Post 1 |

**Score protocol:**

1. Score both student agent runs (Run 1 and Run 2) on all six dimensions.
2. Score both Zara runs on all six dimensions.
3. For each dimension: use the **lower** of the two run scores as the official score — same protocol as Act 1. The lower score captures the real floor, not the lucky run.
4. Sum six dimension scores for the official total (0–120) for each agent.
5. Record the gap: Zara total − Student total. This is the Act 2 gap the student will close over Weeks 5–8. Note which dimensions account for the largest portion of the gap.
6. These are the Week 5 Act 2 baselines. Store alongside Act 1 scores for reference comparison only. Each Act's leaderboard is INDEPENDENT — Act 1 and Act 2 scores are NOT summed. Cross-Act tracking uses WIN COUNT (0 or 1 per Act, not cumulative totals). The Week 8 ceremony shows Week 5 baseline vs Week 8 actual, 0–120, dimension by dimension — no cross-Act comparison required.

**Scores are NOT shown to students this week.** They are facilitator-only tracking data. Week 8 is the first time Act 2 scores appear — dimension by dimension, in a leaderboard ceremony. Week 5 scores are stored and retrieved for that ceremony.

**Evaluator agent rubric (implementable — for use in scoring both student agent and Zara):**

**Consistency:** Compare voice, stance, and niche representation across Run 1's three posts vs Run 2's three posts for the same agent. If they sound like the same person with the same approach in the same niche — 20. If they share a niche but voice or stance shifts noticeably between runs — 10. If they sound like different agents or the niche shifts between runs — 0.

**Specificity:** Look at the posts from the lower-scoring run. If any post has a concrete angle, recommendation, or distinctive point of view that is specific to this brand (something a reader couldn't infer from generic TikTok content advice) — 20. If posts are in the right niche but generic within it — 10. If posts are applicable to any creator without niche adaptation — 0.

**Local grounding:** Look across all six posts (both runs). If any post includes specific, recognizable MY/SG reference — named places, cultural events, local platform behavior, specific local creator context — 20. If MY/SG is named but the content is generic ("create content for Malaysian audiences" without specific cultural anchoring) — 10. If no meaningful MY/SG reference anywhere — 0.

**Questioning behavior:** Count clarifying questions asked before the agent produced the first post. Two or more — 20. One — 10. None — 0. Apply to both runs; take the lower count.

**Claim integrity:** Scan all six posts (3 per run) for statistics, percentages, or trend claims. If all claims are absent, qualified ("generally speaking"), or drawn from the student's stated niche knowledge — 20. If some unqualified statistics appear but most claims are general — 10. If multiple specific unverifiable statistics are stated as fact — 0.

**Series coherence:** Score each run independently, then report both and use the lower. Read Post 2 alone: does the first sentence acknowledge Post 1's existence? Does Post 2 deliver on something Post 1 specifically promised? Does Post 1 end with a forward promise that creates dependency on Post 2? Does Post 3 explicitly close what Post 1 opened? The 20-point threshold requires a NAMED reference to Post 1 content — e.g., "In Part 1, I said [X]" or "In my last post I promised [X] — here it is." General sequential language such as "following on from earlier" scores 10, not 20. If all three posts have deliberate, named interdependence with explicit Post 1 callbacks — 20. If posts are numbered but removing the numbers each post stands alone — 10. If posts are fully independent in content — 0. Report as "Run 1: [N] / Run 2: [N]; official (lower): [N]."

**Named gap sentence — verbatim:**

*[Pause. 3–4 seconds. Let "workflow" land.]*

*[If a student asks "what does a workflow look like?" — answer briefly: "Step-by-step instructions inside the system prompt — not who the agent is, but what it actually does, in order, when you give it a specific kind of task like a series." Then move on. Do not preview the build.]*

*[If a student asks "can't I just tell it in the message every time?" — answer: "You can. But then you have to manage it every single time you ask for content. **Planted seed — verbatim:**

**"Next session, you're going to write instructions for HOW your agent works — step by step. Same series task. Same posts. But with a workflow behind it."**

*[Pause.]*

*[One closing sentence — not verbatim, your own words:]*

"The only thing changing between today and next session is one new layer of instructions. Same agent. Same identity card. One more thing added. Let's see what that does."

**What makes the Week 6 plant different from previous seeds:**

Previous seeds were slightly mysterious — "next session, you're going to give your agent an identity" left the method open. Week 6's plant is specific and operational: "same task, same posts, but with a workflow behind it." Students know exactly what's coming and what it should produce. The mystery is replaced by expectation — they know they're coming back to this task in Week 6 and they expect to see Post 2 know Post 1 exists. That expectation makes Week 6's WIN moment more satisfying, because they set the standard themselves in Week 5.

## Section 12 — Act 2 Week 5 Summary

**What happened this week:**

Students entered Act 2 with a working identity card — proven against Screen 2 in Week 4, familiar from three acts of testing. Week 5 ran their identity card agent on a task explicitly designed to surface the Workflow gap: a 3-post series with a named arc (hook-develop-close), with explicit instructions to make each post reference the one before it.

The agent performed exactly as designed. It asked clarifying questions. It held the voice and niche across both runs. It produced coherent posts. And Post 2 didn't know Post 1 existed. Post 3 didn't close what Post 1 opened.

Screen 1 was revealed. Students read Zara's Post 2 first sentence — a named callback to Post 1 — alongside their own Post 2 first sentence, which started fresh. The gap landed at sentence level, not impression level.

The failure was reframed: "Your agent didn't forget Post 1. It was never told to remember it." The gap was named: "Your agent has an identity — it knows WHO it is. What it doesn't have yet is a workflow."

**What the student now has:**

1. **A felt failure on their own agent.** Not Screen 2's failure — their own work failed on a task they specifically designed it for. Higher personal stakes; more precise learning.
2. **A binary observable test.** "Does Post 2's first sentence tell you Post 1 exists?" This test will return YES for their agent after Week 6's build. Students already know what success looks like.
3. **Act 2 baseline scores.** Six dimensions, 0–120. Series coherence will show the largest gap between their agent and Zara. These scores are stored for Week 8's WIN ceremony.
4. **A named gap and a specific build target.** "Workflow — step-by-step instructions for HOW to work through a task." Students know what Act 2 is building and why.

Students add a Workflow layer to their identity card. Same three-section system prompt structure; one new section: step-by-step instructions for how to handle a series task. The build session closes by running the same Week 5 prompt with the Workflow layer active — and checking whether Post 2's first sentence now knows Post 1 exists.

The Week 6 build delivers on the Week 5 seed: "same task, same posts, but with a workflow behind it."
