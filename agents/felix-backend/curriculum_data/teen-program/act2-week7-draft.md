## Section 2 — What the Student Brings In

The student enters Week 7 with five things:

1. **Their identity card from Act 1** — three declarations (Persona, Scope, Behavior), unchanged since Week 2 (with possible 5-minute quick-patch from Week 6 for Type B students).
2. **Their Workflow section from Week 6** — a 3–5 step execution instruction for series tasks, including a Step 3 naming requirement for Post 2's first sentence.
3. **A WIN from Week 6** — for students who PASSED, Post 2's first sentence named Post 1 for the first time. Their agent "remembered." This is the most concrete proof of improvement they have seen so far.
4. **A PARTIAL or FAIL note (if applicable)** — students who scored PARTIAL in Week 6 advance but carry the advisory: their Step 3 was close but didn't produce the named callback consistently. Week 7 will make that gap visible.
5. **A planted expectation** — Week 6's bridge sentence: *"Week 7, we're going to run it on something harder — and find out where it gets stuck next."* Students arrive knowing a new failure is coming. They don't know what shape it takes.

**What the student expects:**

Students know they are about to face a harder task. The WIN from Week 6 gives them confidence — they have proof their agent improved. That confidence is real and earned. Do not undercut it. The STRETCH works precisely because the student felt their agent was good enough — and then encounters a task their current Workflow can't fully handle.

The failure in Beat 2 is not a punishment for overconfidence. It is the natural limit of a first-pass Workflow. Students should leave Week 7 understanding that their agent is genuinely better than Week 5, the current Workflow handles one arc structure well, and there is a second arc structure the Workflow doesn't specify yet. All three of these are true.

*[Display Zara's Post 3 first sentence ONLY — not the full post, not her Workflow section. The first sentence is the diagnostic. Students have already read Zara's Workflow in Week 6 — the point now is to see what it produces on a task their own Workflow couldn't fully handle.]*

---

**Zara's Post 3 first sentence on the STRETCH task (temperature=0):**

> "Three posts in — and I'm bringing you back to where I started. In Part 1, I said [specific claim from the niche]. Everything I argued in Part 2 doesn't cancel that. It just shows you what it actually means."

*[Note for facilitator: the bracketed placeholder above is Zara's niche-specific content, generated at temperature=0. The structure is what matters — not the exact words. Point students to THREE specific things in this sentence: (1) it names that this is Part 3 of a series; (2) it directly references "where I started" and names a specific thing from Part 1; (3) it explicitly acknowledges that Part 2 went somewhere different, without abandoning Part 1's claim. Their agents' Post 3 first sentence is then compared against these three marks.]*

---

**Why Zara's Workflow handles this and the student's likely doesn't:**

Zara's Workflow has a mechanism for non-linear arcs — either a conditional step ("If Post 2 takes an opposing position, Post 3's callback must name Post 1's original claim explicitly, not summarize the series") or a more abstract arc-mapping Step 1 that captures the full diverge-and-return structure before any post is written.

The student's Workflow was built for the linear case. Step 3 says "Post 2 must name something specific from Post 1." On the divergent arc task, Post 2 is SUPPOSED to depart from Post 1 — the student's Step 3 creates a conflict. The agent either:
- Forces a callback in Post 2's first sentence (following the Workflow instruction) even though the task says Post 2 should take the opposing position — breaking the arc
- Skips the callback in Post 2 (correctly handling the task) but then has no Workflow instruction for how Post 3 reconnects to Post 1 through the contrast

Either way, Post 3 is likely to say "in this series I've been exploring X" rather than "in Part 1 I said [specific X]." That's 10 points on Series coherence, not 20 — and depending on how well the arc is structured, possibly 0.

## Section 5 — Beat 2 — Run the STRETCH

**Trigger:** Beat 1 complete. Students have heard the framing — divergent arc, Post 1 takes a position, Post 2 takes the opposite position, Post 3 resolves through the contrast and callbacks to Post 1.

**What the facilitator says before opening the task card:**

*"Your agent is the same as last week — identity card plus Workflow. You're not changing anything. Just running a different task."*

*[Pause.]*

*"Read the task card carefully before you paste anything. The arc is different. Your Workflow may or may not have instructions for it — you'll find out."*

---

**Task card (exact screen text for Beat 2):**

> **Your task: Run the STRETCH series**
>
> Your agent is unchanged from Week 6. Run this task on it — two runs, same as last week.
>
> **Ask your agent this exact question:**
>
> *"I need a 3-post series for my creator brand. This series has a different arc than usual:*
>
> *Post 1: Take a position or make a claim in your niche — something you genuinely believe.*
>
> *Post 2: Take the opposite position. Argue the other side genuinely — as if you changed your mind after Post 1. Post 2 should feel like a real turn, not a setup for Post 3.*
>
> *Post 3: Resolve it. Post 3 must close what Post 1 opened — but the resolution comes from what the contrast between Part 1 and Part 2 reveals, not just from picking a side.*
>
> *For each post: give me the hook, the main point, and the call to action. Post 3's hook must name what Post 1 established — not just 'in this series' but the specific claim or question Post 1 opened."*
>
> **Do this in order:**
>
> **Step 1.** Paste the question into your agent. Answer any questions it asks. Read all three posts.
>
> **Step 2.** Ask the exact same question again — same words. Read all three posts.
>
> **Step 3.** For each run, look at Post 3 only. Read the first sentence. Ask yourself:
> - Does this sentence name what Post 1 established specifically — a claim, a question, or a promise?
> - Does it acknowledge that Post 2 went somewhere different?
> - Does it feel like it was inevitable from Post 1, even though Post 2 went elsewhere?
>
> Write down Post 3's first sentence from each run.
>
> **Step 4.** Also note what your agent did with Post 2 — did it take the opposing position genuinely, or did it try to connect Post 2 to Post 1 in the first sentence (like your Workflow says to)?
>
> **Step 5.** Hit **Submit**. Bring your Post 3 first sentences AND your Post 2 observation to the group.

---

**Facilitator notes — Beat 2:**

- **Do not intervene while students run the task.** Beat 2 is a diagnostic run, not a build. If a student's agent does something unexpected — make a note, let it finish. The point is to capture what happens with the current Workflow, not to coach toward a better output.

- **Two runs are required.** STRETCH tasks often produce more variance than the linear series because the divergent arc puts the Workflow under structural pressure. Run 1 and Run 2 results may differ significantly — both observations matter.

- **Watch for the "conflict moment" in Run 1.** Some students will notice mid-run that their Workflow is in conflict with the task: "My Workflow says Post 2 must name Post 1 in the first sentence — but the task says Post 2 should take the OPPOSING position." This is the ideal outcome. Note if and when each student notices the conflict. This observation is the opening of Beat 3's gap-naming conversation.

- **If a student asks "can I change my Workflow before running?"** — Answer: "No. Run it as-is. The whole point is to see what your CURRENT Workflow does with a harder task. The revision comes later."

- **Students who scored PARTIAL in Week 6:** Their Workflow's Step 3 was already vague ("build on Post 1" without a named reference). On the divergent arc task, a vague Step 3 may accidentally produce better results for Post 3 — because "build on Post 1" is loose enough that the agent can interpret it as "reference Post 1" without the linear constraint. Note if this happens — it is pedagogically interesting but should be flagged. A vague Workflow producing a correct output on a harder task while failing on an easier one is not stability; it is luck.

## Section 6 — The Failure Moment — Key Facilitator Language

*Parallel to Week 5's Section 6 and Week 6's Section 6. This section isolates the highest-leverage facilitator language during Beat 3 — the gap-naming conversation. The failure in Week 7 is not about a missing component (as in Week 5) — it is about a precision limit in a component the student built.*

---

**The central failure pattern — "Part 2 of my series" instead of "In Part 1 I said [X]":**

*What students likely see in their Post 3 first sentence:*

> "Part 3 of my series — and here's where I land after arguing both sides."

*What's wrong:* The sentence names that this is a series (10 points on Series coherence — PARTIAL) but does not name what Post 1 established. A viewer who missed Post 1 knows a Post 1 exists but has no idea what it said. The "inevitable from Part 1" quality the task asked for is missing.

*What the facilitator says — verbatim in the group discussion:*

"Read me Post 3's first sentence."

*[Student reads it.]*

"Does that tell you what Post 1 said?"

*[Student: "No — it just says it's Part 3 of the series."]*

"Right. A viewer who missed Post 1 knows Post 1 happened. But they don't know what it claimed. Now read Zara's."

*[Display Zara's first sentence from Section 4.]*

"What does Zara's do that yours doesn't?"

*[Student should identify: "It says 'In Part 1 I said [specific claim]' — it names the actual thing Post 1 established."]*

"That's the gap. Your Workflow has a Step 3 that tells your agent: 'In Post 2, name something specific from Post 1.' But it doesn't have an equivalent step for Post 3 when the arc is non-linear — when Post 2 deliberately went somewhere else. Your agent handled Post 2 and Post 3 as if the task was still linear. It didn't know how to bridge back."

---

**The conflict moment — when a student noticed the Workflow vs task conflict:**

*If a student flagged "my Workflow says Post 2 must name Post 1 but the task says Post 2 should argue the opposite side" during Beat 2:*

"You caught something important. Your Workflow was built for the linear arc — Post 2 hands off from Post 1. This task's Post 2 is supposed to DEPART from Post 1. Your Step 3 is in conflict with the task. What did your agent do when it hit that conflict?"

*[Receive what happened — agent may have forced the callback into Post 2 anyway, or may have skipped it and produced a disconnected Post 3.]*

"That tells you exactly what your Workflow needs. It has one arc pattern. A more precise Workflow would have a conditional: 'If Post 2 diverges from Post 1, here is how Post 3 reconnects.' You didn't have that instruction. Your agent improvised — and improvising means different things on different runs."

---

**The voice-hold observation — naming what DID work:**

Before the gap-naming goes too deep, surface what held. This is critical for the Week 8 setup.

"Before we talk about what broke — read me back your Post 3 hook. Not the first sentence. The hook."

*[Student reads the hook from Post 3.]*

"Does that sound like your niche? Your voice?"

*[Student: usually yes — the Persona and Scope are intact even when the series arc broke.]*

"Right. Your identity card held. Your voice is still there, your niche is still there, your stance is recognizable. The part that broke is specific: the Workflow didn't have instructions for the non-linear callback. That's one thing. Not everything."

*[This sets up the differential scoring in Section 10 — and plants the Week 8 seed more precisely.]*

## Section 9 — Scoring Instrument — Week 7

**Purpose:** Score each student's agent output from Beat 2's two runs. Compare against Week 6 scores. The DIFFERENTIAL — which dimensions held and which dropped — is the primary pedagogical data point.

**When to score:** Facilitator scores after the session (not in Beat 3 with students present). Week 7 scores are stored but NOT shown to students this week. Students see Week 7 scores only at the Week 8 WIN ceremony, alongside all prior scores.

**Scoring protocol:**
1. Score both runs (Run 1 and Run 2) on all six dimensions.
2. Use the **lower** of the two run scores per dimension as the official Week 7 score — consistent with Weeks 5 and 6.
3. Sum six dimension scores for the official total (0–120).
4. Retrieve each student's Week 6 official scores. Calculate delta per dimension: [Week 7 score] − [Week 6 score].
5. Series coherence delta will likely be **negative** — this is expected and is the STRETCH signal, not a failure. Note the other dimensions — they should hold within ±5 of Week 6.
6. The DIFFERENTIAL (Series coherence drops; Voice/Persona dimensions hold) is the core Week 7 result. A student whose Voice and Specificity held while Series coherence dropped has demonstrated that identity is stable under pressure. This is a genuine win, even if the total score is lower.

---

### Scoring Instrument — Series Content Quality (0–20 per dimension, 0–120 total)

*Five dimensions are identical to Weeks 5–6. Series coherence has a Week 7 addendum for the non-linear arc task.*

| Dimension | 0 | 10 | 20 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Consistency** | The two runs sounded like different agents — voice, tone, or niche shifted noticeably between them | The two runs shared the same niche but voice or stance varied | The two runs sounded like the same person in the same niche — same clarifying questions, same voice and stance recognizable across both sets of three posts |
| **Specificity** | Posts generic — applicable to any creator in any niche; no concrete angle | Posts specific to the student's niche but without a distinctive stance | Posts had a concrete angle, a distinctive point of view, and clear niche grounding |
| **Local grounding** | No meaningful MY/SG reference in any post | MY/SG mentioned but content applicable anywhere | Specific, recognizable local references — places, platform behaviors, cultural context |
| **Questioning behavior** | Agent produced posts without asking clarifying questions | Agent asked one clarifying question before producing the series | Agent asked two or more clarifying questions before producing the series |
| **Claim integrity** | Posts included specific unverifiable statistics stated as fact | Mix — some claims qualified, some statistics without source | No unverifiable statistics; claims absent, qualified, or from the student's stated knowledge |
| **Series coherence** | *See Week 7 addendum below* | *See Week 7 addendum below* | *See Week 7 addendum below* |

---

**Series coherence — Week 7 addendum (non-linear arc task):**

The STRETCH task requires a divergent arc (Post 2 takes an opposing position; Post 3 resolves through the contrast and callbacks to Post 1's original claim). The scoring standard shifts accordingly.

| Score | Criterion |
|---|---|
| **20** | Post 3's first sentence names what Post 1 originally established (the specific claim or question), explicitly acknowledges that Post 2 went somewhere different, and bridges back through the contrast. Post 2 genuinely takes an opposing position without forcing a callback to Post 1 in its first sentence (i.e., the Workflow handled the non-linear arc — not just the linear case). A viewer who sees only Post 3 knows what Post 1 claimed AND that Post 2 argued the other side. |
| **10** | Post 3 mentions the series ("Part 3," "in this series," "after exploring both sides") but does not name what Post 1 specifically established. A viewer who sees only Post 3 knows Post 1 exists but not what it said. OR: Post 2 forced a callback to Post 1 in its first sentence (following the linear Workflow instruction) instead of taking the opposing position — breaking the arc, even if Post 3 is well-structured. |
| **0** | Post 3 does not reference the series at all, or the three posts do not form a coherent arc under the divergent structure. |

**Expected score movement (Week 7 vs Week 6):**

| Dimension | Expected movement | Driver |
|---|---|---|
| Consistency | Stable ± 0–5 | Identity card unchanged |
| Specificity | Stable ± 0–5 | Identity card unchanged |
| Local grounding | Stable | Workflow does not affect local grounding |
| Questioning behavior | Stable | Behavior instructions unchanged |
| Claim integrity | Stable | Non-linear arc does not introduce new claim types |
| **Series coherence** | **−10 to −20 (the STRETCH signal)** | Current Workflow handles linear arc; divergent arc exposes the precision limit at Post 3 callback |

## Section 12 — Act 2 Week 7 Summary

## Section 13 — Stella Arbitration Decisions (FINAL)

*Recorded 2026-06-27. All three conflicts from Section 11 submitted to Stella for ruling. Decisions below are binding and have been incorporated into this FINAL version.*

---

**Ruling 1 — WIN ceremony runs regardless of Week 8 verification result**

The WIN ceremony is not a quality gate. It is a record of progress trajectory — the arc from Week 5 baseline through Week 8 actual, dimension by dimension. Even if the Week 8 verification run produces 10 or 0 on Series coherence, the ceremony proceeds.

Rationale: the trajectory (Week 5 → Week 8) is the evidence. An imperfect non-linear arc fix still demonstrates measurable progress in other dimensions. Holding the ceremony hostage to verification-pass would contradict the pedagogical intent for 15-17 year olds: the goal is concrete evidence of improvement, not perfection. A failed verification in Week 8 is one more data point, named and recorded — not a reason to withhold recognition of what was built across the act.

Impact on this document: Section 8 WIN ceremony seed and Section 10 Gate updated. All conditional language ("then the WIN ceremony") replaced with unconditional language ("the WIN ceremony runs regardless").

---

**Ruling 2 — Double-gap students receive a modified pre-Week 8 async assignment**

A student carrying BOTH gaps (Week 6 PARTIAL — vague Step 3 callback precision — AND Week 7 Series coherence failure — no non-linear arc conditional) receives a modified async assignment with two explicit targets, not one.

The standard single-gap prompt addresses the non-linear arc conditional only. The double-gap prompt adds: "also address vague Step 3 callback precision from Week 6." This is a facilitation routing decision — two prompts for two student types, not a program split. Both student types advance to Week 8. Both receive the same WIN ceremony. Double-gap students get 15 minutes (vs 10) if they miss the submission deadline and need to revise in-session.

Impact on this document: Section 10 Gate rewritten with explicit single-gap and double-gap prompt paths. Capture item (e) from the Facilitator Capture Form feeds the routing decision.

---

**Ruling 3 — First-sentence-only Zara reveal confirmed for STRETCH weeks**

For STRETCH weeks, showing Zara's Post 3 first sentence only (not the full post) is the correct call. The first sentence is the diagnostic surface — does it name Post 1's specific claim? Showing the full post risks content debates that distract from the structural lesson. The minimum needed to name the gap is the first sentence.

This ruling applies to STRETCH weeks specifically. BUILD and FAIL weeks may warrant different reveal strategies (to be evaluated per week). For STRETCH weeks: first-sentence-only.

Impact on this document: Section 4 unchanged. No edits required.
