# Audience Levels — Corporate Track

Corporate content is always calibrated to the audience level of the cohort.
This reference defines what changes at each level.

Audience level is an independent variable from industry context. Both must be
set at intake.

---

## The Four Levels

### Management
*Who:* Team leads, department heads, middle managers, senior individual
contributors with budget or decision authority.

*What changes in content design:*
- **Process complexity:** Higher. Managers own multi-step processes with
  downstream dependencies (e.g. weekly reporting that feeds board packs,
  performance review aggregation, resource allocation tracking).
- **Failure stakes:** The engineered failure should have visible business
  consequences — not just "the output looks wrong," but "a decision would be
  made on this."
- **Evaluator expectations:** The evaluator judges against strategic standards.
  It asks: "Would your stakeholders trust this?" and "What happens downstream
  if this is wrong?"
- **Autonomy:** Higher. Less scaffolding, more expectation of independent
  problem-solving.
- **Motivation frame:** Staying ahead, looking capable to leadership, unlocking
  team capacity.

### Admin & Back-office
*Who:* Administrative staff, coordinators, executive assistants, data entry,
back-office operations.

*What changes in content design:*
- **Process complexity:** Medium-low. Processes are often high-volume,
  repetitive, and rule-bound (e.g. invoice processing, calendar management,
  data entry, filing, correspondence drafting).
- **Failure stakes:** The engineered failure should be about volume and accuracy
  — "this would take you all week to check manually" or "one wrong entry
  propagates everywhere."
- **Evaluator expectations:** The evaluator judges against accuracy and
  consistency standards. Format matters a lot.
- **Autonomy:** Medium. Clear scaffolding, but not hand-holding.
- **Motivation frame:** Getting their week back, reducing the boring pile,
  not being the last person doing things manually.

### Frontline & Operations
*Who:* Frontline workers, shop floor staff, customer-facing staff, operations
technicians.

*What changes in content design:*
- **Process complexity:** Lower. Processes are narrow, concrete, and
  immediately observable (e.g. shift handover notes, incident logging, checklist
  completion, customer query routing).
- **Failure stakes:** The engineered failure should be tangible and immediate —
  something the learner can see or feel in their daily work.
- **Evaluator expectations:** Practical, plain-language judgement. "Would this
  work on the floor tomorrow?"
- **Autonomy:** Lower. More scaffolding, shorter tasks, more frequent
  check-ins built into the cycle design.
- **Motivation frame:** Making the shift easier, not falling behind, looking
  competent to the supervisor.
- **Special note:** This audience has the least time for training. Cycles must
  be designed to fit around a demanding schedule — 3–4 hours per week maximum,
  with tasks completable in short bursts.

### Specialist-IC (Individual Contributor)
*Who:* Non-management specialists — marketers, analysts, content creators,
sales representatives, HR officers, project coordinators.

*What changes in content design:*
- **Process complexity:** Medium-high. Processes are knowledge-intensive and
  often involve judgment calls (e.g. weekly content drafts, competitor
  analysis, lead research, project status reports, HR intake processing).
- **Failure stakes:** The engineered failure should expose a quality or
  judgment gap — "you'd share this, but an expert would immediately see the
  problem."
- **Evaluator expectations:** Domain-standard quality. The evaluator sounds
  like a senior peer, not a supervisor.
- **Autonomy:** High. This audience is often self-directed; they will push back
  if content feels beneath them.
- **Motivation frame:** Doing the interesting parts of the job, delegating the
  tedious parts, becoming the person on the team who can build things.

---

## How Audience Level Interacts with Industry

Industry context sets the *scenario and vocabulary.* Audience level sets the
*complexity, scaffolding depth, and evaluator expectations.* Both must be set
independently.

Example: a retail cohort of managers needs retail-specific scenarios (inventory
reporting, store performance packs) judged to management-level standards.
A retail cohort of frontline staff needs retail-specific scenarios (shift
handover, customer query logging) designed for shorter tasks and more scaffolding.
Same industry, very different content.
